# DAVID SHEDD: CHINA'S SPIES — COMPREHENSIVE ANALYSIS

## I. Shedd's Background & Credentials

David Shedd served a long tenure in the intelligence community, with early experiences shaped by his upbringing in South America during the Cold War, specifically witnessing the effects of socialism under Salvador Allende in Chile. His intrigue in global affairs led him to join the CIA in 1982, contributing to U.S. foreign policy efforts particularly in Central America. Shedd's career in the intelligence community culminated in his role as Acting Director of the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA), providing him with profound insights into geopolitical dynamics and intelligence operations.

## II. The "Great Heist" Thesis

Shedd's book, "The Great Heist: China's Epic Campaign to Steal America's Secrets," posits that China's Ministry of State Security (MSS) has pursued a large-scale espionage campaign to acquire technological and economic advantages from the United States. The thesis underscores China's strategic use of economic espionage as a state policy reflecting its ambition for global leadership, pursuing this objective systematically by exploiting globalization and the interconnectedness of technology industries.

## III. Industrial & Economic Espionage

Shedd identifies industrial espionage as a primary method through which China aims to increase its global competitiveness. This includes theft of intellectual property, trade secrets, and technology innovations. Shedd highlights China's focus on sectors outlined in the Made in China 2025 strategy, targeting industries vital for future economic dominance including biotech, stealth technology, and hypersonics. He mentions the significant financial cost of such espionage to the U.S., quoting former FBI director Chris Wray's assessment of approximately $600 billion per year in losses.

## IV. The MSS & 18th Bureau

The MSS is portrayed as a complex, effective organization tasked with both foreign and domestic intelligence operations, with the 18th Bureau specifically focused on operations against the United States. This Bureau is self-contained, capable of executing cyber operations, leveraging companies for industrial espionage, and engaging in clandestine activities without the same legal constraints faced by U.S. counterparts. Shedd discusses how the MSS operates synergistically with the PLA, utilizing Chinese citizens and companies globally under the 2017 National Intelligence Law that mandates cooperation with state intelligence operations.

## V. National Intelligence Estimate

Shedd refers to a National Intelligence Estimate from the early 2000s, which challenged the expectation that engaging China through economic means such as WTO membership would lead to political reform or compliance with international norms. The intelligence community was skeptical about China's willingness to democratize or genuinely adhere to global rules, which Shedd believes was a critical oversight.

## VI. Battlefield Consequences

The technological advancements achieved by China through espionage have tangible battlefield implications. Shedd discusses the theft of technologies related to the U.S.'s precision strike capabilities, such as the F-35 stealth aircraft, and China's progress in hypersonic weapons. These developments, facilitated by espionage, potentially diminish U.S. military technological edges and have critical implications for global power dynamics and military strategy.

## VII. Counterarguments & Caveats

Shedd addresses and refutes the perception that his views are xenophobic, clarifying that his critique targets the policies of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) rather than the Chinese people. Opposition to his views often stems from allegations of unfairly maligning Chinese individuals and questioning the motives behind focusing on Chinese espionage, suggesting ulterior geopolitical motivations. Shedd emphasizes the need for recognizing the CCP's strategic intentions without broadly stigmatizing all Chinese citizens.

## VIII. Key Cases & Examples Mentioned

1. **F-35 Aircraft Technology Theft**: Highlighted as a quintessential example of China's successful espionage, costing the U.S. hundreds of millions in counterintelligence measures to remedy compromised systems.
   
2. **Microsoft and National Laboratories Exposure**: References to vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure, with Chinese entities embedded in technologically significant projects such as cloud computing and working within national laboratories like Los Alamos.

3. **PLA Capabilities**: The successful test of hypersonic missiles exemplifies advanced strategic weapons China has developed potentially using stolen U.S. technology.

## IX. Comparison to Eftimiades' 900-Case Framework

Shedd's discussion dovetails with Nicholas Eftimiades' framework which catalogs hundreds of Chinese espionage cases, collectively illustrating a persistent and wide-ranging effort to siphon U.S. technological advancements. Shedd’s approach amplifies this framework by providing contemporary examples and drawing direct lines between espionage activities and strategic outcomes for the PLA and CCP.

## X. Strategic Implications

The broader strategic implications of China's espionage are profound. Shedd argues that the cumulative effect of intellectual property theft not only narrows the technological gap but also shifts tactical and strategic advantages. The embeddedness of MSS operations within the Chinese state's economic ambitions presents a formidable challenge to U.S. national security and demands comprehensive policy responses to safeguard innovations and maintain military superiority. The interactions between espionage activities and broader geopolitical strategies underpin a new era of economic and technological conflict shaping the global order.
