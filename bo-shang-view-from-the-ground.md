# Bo Shang: On the Ground, from China's Perspective

A counter-view weighing Nicholas Eftimiades' SpyCast framing against what things actually
look like from where I sit — Massachusetts, 2026, having spent a lot of time in both worlds.

---

## Half as Bad as That Guy Makes It Out to Be

Eftimiades ran a 34-year career inside US intelligence. He's good. His database of ~900 cases is
real. But he's also selling a book, doing the podcast circuit, and every interview follows the
same arc: monolithic threat, inexhaustible scale, existential stakes. That's the genre. Nobody
invites the former counterintelligence officer on to say "actually, most of this is manageable."

Half as bad means:

- **900 cases over multiple decades** is not an avalanche. The FBI opens more domestic
  investigations in a single year. The 900 cases span the 1980s to 2025 — call it 20–25
  cases per year across the entire world. If China's "whole-of-society" machine were as
  frictionless as portrayed, the case count would be in the tens of thousands.

- **The "bad tradecraft" cases are the majority.** Su Jianfeng trying to buy radiation-hardened
  chips with a car-application cover story. Kevin Mallory copying classified docs at a FedEx.
  Jerry Lee writing true names in a notebook left in a hotel room. These aren't terrifying;
  they're Keystone Cops. The few genuinely sophisticated operations (Dixon Yeo, Christine Fang)
  are outliers, not the median.

- **China's intelligence services are perpetually under-resourced** relative to ambition.
  Xi's 2015 cyber transfer out of the PLA happened because PLA units were *stealing from
  Chinese companies and selling to competitors*. That's not a well-oiled machine; that's a
  corruption problem big enough to force a top-level reorganization. The MSS contracting
  cyber out to private security companies isn't strategic genius — it's outsourcing because
  they can't staff it internally.

- **"Exceeds all other countries combined"** is an assertion without a denominator. What's
  the basis of comparison? Budget? Personnel count? Cases opened? The US intelligence
  community's budget alone ($100B+) likely exceeds China's entire military R&D spend.
  Scale claims without methodology are vibes dressed as analysis.

## Half as Good as Being Left Alone — or More for Me

I live in Massachusetts. I write code, publish research, run open-source projects. Some of my
repos are about PLA weapons analysis (OSINT, publicly sourced). Some are about cancer drug
discovery. Some are AI agent frameworks. I've contributed to understanding Chinese espionage
publicly, including this very repository.

What I want from the Chinese government is simple: **leave me alone.**

That's not a high bar. I'm not a dissident. I'm not organizing. I'm not a threat. I'm a
programmer with opinions and a GitHub account. The Chinese government has approximately zero
reason to care about me — unless someone decides to care, in which case the machinery
Eftimiades describes (the MSS, the consulate case officers, the diaspora infiltration) becomes
relevant to my life in a way I'd rather it not.

"Half as good as being left alone" means:

- The best-case scenario is invisibility. The Chinese state doesn't notice me, doesn't
  catalog me, doesn't run my name through any database. I'm noise. That's already half
  the battle won.

- "Or more for me" — meaning: if the choice is between being noticed and being left alone,
  take the latter every time. Being noticed by the MSS means either recruitment, surveillance,
  or worse. I know the case studies. I've read the transcripts. I've seen what happens to
  people who become dots on their map.

- This isn't paranoia. It's pattern recognition. The Causeway Bookstore owners published books
  about Xi Jinping's family and got renditioned. Christine Fang wasn't a dissident — she was a
  student association leader who became a multi-decade political infiltration asset. Wang Shujun
  spied on democracy activists inside their own NGO for 15 years. You don't have to be the target
  to get caught in the net. You just have to be nearby when they cast it.

## The Uncomfortable Middle

The truth sits between Eftimiades' maximum-threat framing and the "nothing to see here" dismissal
that Beijing's state media would prefer. It's this:

1. Chinese espionage is a real and persistent problem — but it's half as organized, half as
   capable, and half as successful as the worst-case narrative claims.

2. The "whole-of-society" model is real policy but sloppy execution. Most of it is hotels
   forwarding guest lists and SOEs filing pro-forma reports nobody reads. The scary cases
   make the news; the 10,000 boring ones don't.

3. For someone like me — overseas, technically capable, with a public footprint — the calculus
   is simple: don't become a case study. Keep your head down. Ship code. Let the professionals
   on both sides do their thing while you stay out of the crossfire.

4. Being left alone by the PRC is worth more than half of anything else. It's the baseline
   requirement for everything else to work. If that holds, the research continues, the repos
   stay public, and Eftimiades' book becomes another data point rather than a personal
   instruction manual.
