# COMPREHENSIVE ANALYSIS: CHINESE ESPIONAGE THROUGH 900 CASES

## I. STRATEGIC PHILOSOPHY & DOCTRINE

### CCP Protection as Primary Mission
The overarching philosophy of China's intelligence services is centered around the protection of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP). This internal focus is paramount, with all intelligence activities being secondary to the primary objective of safeguarding the party's interests.

### Sun Tzu's Art of War Influence
The influence of Sun Tzu's "The Art of War" is deeply embedded in China's national security strategy. A famous quote, "the person who wins without fighting is the acme of skill," encapsulates the Chinese approach to strategic dominance through non-combative means. This strategy emphasizes achieving objectives through control rather than confrontation.

### "Unrestricted Warfare" Concept
China's approach includes elements of "unrestricted warfare," where traditional boundaries of conflict are transcended. This includes manipulating media, political influencers, and financial systems to achieve strategic goals without conventional military engagement.

### "Chen Diyu" / "Fish on the Bottom of the Ocean"
Mao Zedong's doctrine of placing long-term strategic assets abroad is embodied in the term "Chen Diyu," or "fish on the bottom of the ocean." This approach refers to embedding operatives who can remain dormant for years, awaiting activation for intelligence-gathering or influence operations.

### Whole-of-Society Approach vs. Western Siloed Agencies
China's intelligence methodology is characterized by a whole-of-society approach, in which various societal elements, including state-owned and private enterprises, are mobilized for intelligence purposes. This contrasts sharply with the more compartmentalized and agency-specific intelligence operations in Western nations.

## II. ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURE

### Ministry of State Security (MSS) - Primary Foreign Intelligence
The MSS is China's main foreign intelligence service, responsible for collecting and analyzing information relevant to China's global interests and national security.

### Ministry of Public Security (MPS) - National Police
The MPS serves as China's national police force, and under Xi Jinping, its cooperation with the MSS has increased, fostering greater synergy in domestic and international espionage activities.

### People's Liberation Army (PLA) - Cyber Role Until 2015
Before 2015, the PLA was heavily involved in cyber espionage. However, due to issues of corruption and inefficiency, this role was transferred to the MSS.

### United Front Work Department (UFWD)
The UFWD is a critical component of China's intelligence operations, focusing on influencing individuals and organizations outside China to align with CCP interests. Its activities include political manipulation and fostering pro-China sentiment globally.

### State-Owned Enterprises and Private Companies Conscripted
Approximately 150,000 state-owned enterprises and select private companies are legally obliged to support Chinese intelligence efforts. This involves gathering intelligence and facilitating covert operations through business interactions.

### Xi Jinping's Reforms
Under Xi Jinping, significant reforms were implemented to enhance the coordination and efficiency of China's intelligence services. These include the reassignment of cyber responsibilities from the PLA to the MSS and the promotion of collaboration between the MSS and MPS.

## III. TARGETS & PRIORITIES (from ~900 cases)

### Primary Targets
- **United States**: Focus on both national security and economic espionage.
- **Taiwan**: Represents half of all national security-related espionage cases.

### Secondary Targets
- **Europe**: Targeting countries like Germany, France, and Sweden for economic and technological espionage, as well as political manipulation.

### Tactical Shift Post-2019
There has been a notable shift in focus from federal to state and local levels in the US, reflecting a strategic adaptation to influence political figures and systems at these levels.

## IV. TRADECRAFT - CASE STUDIES

### BAD TRADECRAFT

1. **Su Jianfeng**: Attempted to buy radiation-hardened chips for implausible civilian use, setting up an easily detectable cover story and ultimately arrested after a sting operation in Bangkok.

2. **Kevin Mallory**: A former intelligence officer compromised by weak personal tradecraft, including the use of a public FedEx to copy classified documents and an unreliable Chinese-provided destruction app.

3. **Jerry Lee**: Compromised US assets in China by maintaining a notebook with true names of CIA assets, which was discovered during an FBI operation.

### GOOD TRADECRAFT

1. **Dixon Yeo**: Bypassed passport checks, utilized robust operational security such as use of rotating WeChat accounts, and collected sensitive resumes, showing meticulous tradecraft.

2. **Fang Fang (Christine Fang)**: Embedded in US local politics, developing long-term relationships and influence without detection until an FBI intervention.

### COVERT INFLUENCE / POLITICAL MANIPULATION

3. **Mike Sun / Chen Jie**: Attempted political manipulation and influence through local elections with hidden financial backing and strategic placements.

4. **Wang Shujun**: Operated covertly within a Chinese democracy NGO in New York to report on dissidents for the MSS.

### EXTRAORDINARY RENDITIONS

5. **Causeway Bookstore (2015)**: Highlighting China's aggressive extraterritorial reach, owners were abducted from Hong Kong and Thailand for publishing materials deemed threatening to the CCP.

## V. COLLECTION METHODS

- **Human Intelligence**: Encompasses operations in personalized settings such as restaurants and diaspora communities. 
- **Cyber Espionage**: Transitioned to the MSS and supported by Chinese security companies; a vast and advanced cyber capability.
- **SIGINT**: Includes operations out of strategic locations like Cuba and areas with Belt and Road Initiative connections.
- **Economic Espionage**: The exploitation of state-owned enterprises and strict legal frameworks to mandate intelligence cooperation.
- **University and Diaspora Infiltration**: Leveraging academic institutions and diaspora communities for strategic gains.

## VI. CULTURAL & COERCIVE DYNAMICS

### Confucian-Based Subtle Threat Culture
The cultural nuances, such as indirect threats, play a crucial role in recruitment and compliance within intelligence operations.

### Patriotic National Security Education
Chinese citizens receive extensive national security-focused education, fostering a pervasive readiness to support state initiatives from a young age.

### Blend of Belief and Coercion Among Assets
Assets often act under a mix of ideological commitment and coercion, influenced by a potent cultural and governmental reinforcement of national duty.

### Open Email Communications
Demonstrating the normalization of intelligence activities, open communications occur between MSS officers and civilian collaborators in the commercial sector.

## VII. KEY INTELLIGENCE FAILURES & VULNERABILITIES

### 2010 Mass Compromise of CIA Assets in China
A catastrophic breach that severely impacted the CIA’s network in China, hindering future recruitment and operations.

### Long-Term Chilling Effect on Recruitment
The compromise has instilled a pervasive reluctance to engage with US intelligence by local contacts even years later.

### FBI's Late Focus on State-Level Political Targeting
The oversight and late response to Chinese influence at the state level highlight a vulnerability in US counterintelligence efforts.

### Western Structural Inability to Match Whole-of-Society Approach
Western intelligence operations are hindered by a lack of integrated societal mobilization, unlike China's comprehensive use of its populace in intelligence efforts.

## VIII. STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT & FUTURE OUTLOOK

- **Scale of Chinese Espionage**: China's intelligence operations are unmatched globally in scope and scale.
- **Resource Limitations**: Western nations, including the US, face significant challenges in scaling resources to respond effectively to Chinese strategies.
- **Intellectual Property Loss**: This has amounted to trillions of dollars, signifying the vast impact of Chinese economic espionage.
- **Security and Government Structure**: The current structure is insufficient to support and protect corporate and national interests effectively.
- **Organizational Culture Challenge**: Within Western systems, an ingrained organizational culture impedes a unified and effective response strategy.
